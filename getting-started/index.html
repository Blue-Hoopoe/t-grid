Why and when use t-grid?

I have created the t-grid as a result of compromise between old browsers support and responsive design. I was definitely tired of worrying about how Internet Explorer would render my flex-box grid and how could I fix this.

Fortunately for me and you, there is one display property that we can use for our advantage: tables. They sometimes can behave like a flexboxes and they can be a little bit more adjustable then standard divs.

I truly believe that t-grid can be a nice consensus between design and usability in some cases. Just be sure that you know its potential and its wrinkles. It is not suitable for all situations. Hovewer, it is still worth considering.

Pros and cons

Below I present you some of my favorite advantages of using t-grid:

  • Tables are really widely supported.
  • Because of a sass source you can easly customize your grid.
  • You can create contained, nested tables.
  • In contrast to standard floated grid, columns can automatically shrink depending on their content.
  • They are perfect for filled grid layouts with no extra space beetwen.

But there are two sides to every story:

  • Rows can't be wrapped as long as they are displaying as a table.
  • You can face some padding/margin problems.
  • Nested tables can lead to some unwanted effects, so it's recommended to mix table and float grids.
  • To create nested tables you must provide jQuery.

Say hello to t-grid

I have created for you a tutorial in this very folder which includes five lessons to help you begin.

To get started, you just need to have overall concept how float grids work, because t-grid is very similar. In t-grid there are by default five breakpoints to use and twelve columns to your service:

  • xxs - 360px,
  • xs - 576px,
  • sm - 768px,
  • md - 992px,
  • lg - 1200px.

The great thing is that you can easily adapt this numbers for your needs! Just change some variables in source/sass/_variables.scss file and compile the grid.
